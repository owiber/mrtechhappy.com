I'm going to talk some more about PBS now in hopes of finding somebody else who loves their documentaries. A few days ago I watched one about January 28, 1998, a day when some massive waves hit Oahu's North Shore. A few professional big wave surfers managed to get out to the one spot that was actually surfable and ride some 40-80 foot waves. The best part was that some guys with IMAX cameras were there in a helicopter (piloted by a Vietnam war veteran, no less) to film it all. The result was some really cool footage. I am not, however, a fan of the children's shows on PBS, since most of them are either gay love fests ("Teletubbies", "Thomas the train", "Noddy") or make kids really hyper ("Between the Lions", and to a lesser extent "Sesame Street"). I'd say most cartoons are more educational than PBS shows. Anyways, the other day I caught a few minutes from a show about JJ the Jet plane, his friend Big Jake, and the (and this is a direct quote) "special time [they] spend together every night." Usually the programs I catch late at night are real gems, though. Lately they've been showing a strange show called "The Second Voyage of the Mimi", which seems to be about archaeology and ancient Mezoamerican cultures. The kicker is that its main character is none other than a very young Ben Affleck. Probably my favorite educational show on PBS very late at night is "Project Mathematics", a series of episodes created by Caltech to teach Mathematics. For a long time I had dreamt about revolutionizing the way mathematics are taught (in my opinion, public schools go about it very poorly), but this show does almost everything I wanted to, and better than I could have. While I haven't seen very many ones on concepts I haven't learned, it gives the history behind everything they talk about, all to a very well timed classical score. There are very few things I would like more than to speak with the man who is behind the creation of Project Mathematics.
